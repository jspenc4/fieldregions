Population Potential Fields

Visualizing global demographic gravity - revealing how population distribution creates invisible landscapes of human interaction

Seeing the Invisible Structure of Human Geography

What if we could visualize population not as dots on a map, but as a gravitational field? Just as mass creates gravity in physics, concentrations of people create "demographic gravity" - patterns of accessibility, economic potential, and cultural influence that shape our world.

Using the Stewart-Warntz population potential model (developed in the 1950s) with modern global data and computational methods, we can finally see these invisible structures. The 1/d³ distance decay reveals patterns that traditional maps hide: linear ridges along the Nile and Java, cross-border megalopolises, and the polycentric structure of regions like Nigeria.

Key Innovation

By varying the spatial scale (15-mile vs 30-mile minimum distance), we reveal different levels of urban structure - from dense urban cores to regional cultural geography. The mathematics isn't arbitrary: decades of empirical research on migration, trade, and communication flows confirm that human interaction follows this steep distance decay.

Geographic Insights from Global Analysis

  • The Nile Wall: Population potential of 1,090-1,420 forms a continuous north-south ridge following the Nile Valley - one of Earth's most dramatic linear population structures
  • Java's Ridge: 600 miles of continuous dense settlement (potential 920-1,505) across the island - among the most densely populated regions globally
  • Nigeria's Three Peaks: Lagos (Yoruba, 683), Igbo heartland (Owerri/Aba, 590), and Kano (Hausa, 479) perfectly map Nigeria's three major cultural regions
  • Cross-Border Megalopolises: San Diego/Tijuana merge into a single peak - the data sees functional urban regions, not political boundaries
  • Europe's Structure: Istanbul (672) edges out London (643), but the London/Paris/Amsterdam cluster forms Western Europe's dominant ridge through mutual reinforcement
  • Asian Dominance: Top 100 peaks heavily concentrated in South Asia (India/Bangladesh), East Asia (China/Japan), and Southeast Asia (Indonesia/Philippines) - reflecting the global population center

Methodology & Theory

Stewart-Warntz Population Potential Model

The population potential at any location is calculated as the sum of all populations divided by their distance cubed:

Potential(P) = Σ (Population_i / max(distance_i, min_distance)³)

  • Distance Decay (1/d³): Based on empirical studies of migration, trade, and communication patterns from the 1940s-1950s. Human interaction falls off much faster than physical gravity.
  • Minimum Distance: Acts as a spatial scale parameter - 15 miles reveals urban cores, 30 miles reveals regional structure, 100 miles reveals megacity dominance zones.
  • Haversine Distance: Great circle distance on Earth's surface ensures dataset-independent, geographically accurate results.
  • Scale Invariance: Rankings legitimately change with scale - different min_distance values answer different questions about urban structure.

Data Sources

  • World: GPW v4.11 (2020) - Gridded Population of the World at 15 arc-minute (0.25°) resolution, approximately 17 miles at the equator
  • United States: 2020 Census Block Groups - highest resolution publicly available population data
  • Hex Grid Infill: 30-mile hexagonal grid fills ocean and sparse areas to smooth coastlines and create continuous surface
